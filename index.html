Know Your Rights!

Knowledge is power — especially when it comes to your rights as a citizen.

This website is your guide to understanding the U.S. Constitution and its amendments in simple, real-life terms. Learn what each amendment means, see examples, and discover landmark Supreme Court cases that protect your rights.

1. First Amendment – Freedoms of Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, and Petition

  • Freedom of Religion
    Example: You can practice any religion or none at all. Public schools cannot force students to pray.
    Case: Engel v. Vitale (1962) – Mandatory school prayer is unconstitutional.
  • Freedom of Speech
    Example: You can post opinions about government policies on social media or speak at a protest.
    Case: Tinker v. Des Moines (1969) – Students’ symbolic protest was protected.
  • Freedom of the Press
    Example: Newspapers and websites can report on government corruption without censorship.
    Case: New York Times Co. v. United States (1971) – Publication of the Pentagon Papers allowed.
  • Freedom of Assembly
    Example: People can gather peacefully for marches or protests in public spaces.
    Case: De Jonge v. Oregon (1937) – Peaceful political assembly is protected.
  • Right to Petition the Government
    Example: Citizens can submit petitions or emails to lawmakers to change laws.
    Case: McDonald v. Smith (1985) – Citizens can petition without fear of retaliation.

2. Second Amendment – Right to Bear Arms

  • Example: Citizens can legally own firearms for self-defense in states that allow it.
    Case: District of Columbia v. Heller (2008) – Individuals have the right to own firearms at home for self-defense.

3. Third Amendment – Quartering of Soldiers

  • Example: You cannot be forced to let soldiers live in your house during peacetime.
    Case: Engblom v. Carey (1982) – Applied the Third Amendment to state actions.

4. Fourth Amendment – Search and Seizure

  • Example: Police must get a warrant to search your home or phone unless there’s probable cause.
    Case: Mapp v. Ohio (1961) – Evidence obtained illegally cannot be used in court.

5. Fifth Amendment – Rights of the Accused

  • Example: You cannot be forced to testify against yourself in a criminal case.
    Case: Miranda v. Arizona (1966) – Established Miranda rights: “You have the right to remain silent…”

6. Sixth Amendment – Right to a Fair Trial

  • Example: You have the right to a public trial, a lawyer, and to see witnesses against you.
    Case: Gideon v. Wainwright (1963) – States must provide lawyers for defendants who cannot afford one.

7. Seventh Amendment – Trial by Jury in Civil Cases

  • Example: You can have a jury trial in lawsuits involving money or property.
    Case: Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover (1959) – Right to jury trial upheld in combined legal and equitable claims.

8. Eighth Amendment – Limits on Fines and Punishments

  • Example: You cannot be sentenced to life without parole for stealing a small amount.
    Case: Furman v. Georgia (1972) – Arbitrary death penalty sentencing violated the Eighth Amendment.

9. Ninth Amendment – Rights Retained by the People

  • Example: You have privacy rights even if not explicitly listed in the Constitution.
    Case: Griswold v. Connecticut (1965) – Recognized a right to privacy in marital contraception.

10. Tenth Amendment – Powers Reserved to the States or People

  • Example: States can make laws about education, marriage, or local taxes.
    Case: United States v. Lopez (1995) – Limited Congress’ power, reinforcing state authority.
