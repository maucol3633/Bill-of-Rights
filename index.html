Know Your Rights

Knowledge is power — especially when it comes to your rights as a citizen.

Know Your Rights is your go-to resource for understanding the U.S. Constitution and its amendments in simple, real-life terms. Here, you’ll learn:

  • What each amendment means for you
  • Real-world examples of your rights in action
  • Key Supreme Court cases that shape how these rights are protected

Whether you’re a student, a first-time voter, or just curious about the law, our goal is to make the Constitution easy to understand and relevant to your everyday life.

Explore your rights, stay informed, and empower yourself.

First Amendment

Freedom of Religion, Speech, Press, Assembly, and Petition.

Example: You can post opinions about government policies on social media or peacefully protest.

Supreme Court Case: Tinker v. Des Moines (1969)

Second Amendment

Right to Bear Arms.

Example: Citizens can legally own firearms for self-defense.

Supreme Court Case: District of Columbia v. Heller (2008)

Third Amendment

Quartering of Soldiers.

Example: You cannot be forced to house soldiers in peacetime.

Supreme Court Case: Engblom v. Carey (1982)

Fourth Amendment

Search and Seizure.

Example: Police need a warrant to search your home or phone.

Supreme Court Case: Mapp v. Ohio (1961)

Fifth Amendment

Rights of the Accused.

Example: You cannot be forced to testify against yourself.

Supreme Court Case: Miranda v. Arizona (1966)

Sixth Amendment

Right to a Fair Trial.

Example: You are entitled to a lawyer and a public trial.

Supreme Court Case: Gideon v. Wainwright (1963)

Seventh Amendment

Trial by Jury in Civil Cases.

Example: You can have a jury trial in lawsuits involving money or property.

Supreme Court Case: Beacon Theatres, Inc. v. Westover (1959)

Eighth Amendment

Limits on Fines and Punishments.

Example: No cruel or unusual punishment or excessive fines.

Supreme Court Case: Furman v. Georgia (1972)

Ninth Amendment

Rights Retained by the People.

Example: You have privacy rights even if not explicitly listed.

Supreme Court Case: Griswold v. Connecticut (1965)

Tenth Amendment

Powers Reserved to the States or People.

Example: States can make laws about education, marriage, or local taxes.

Supreme Court Case: United States v. Lopez (1995)
